The remembering

In the days of old, when a person lost their way, the elders would say: "Go back to the forest. Walk with the medicines. Listen again."

WILD! Healing is a return to that ancient remembering. It is not simply a program. It is a pilgrimage — a medicine walk across time and continents, guided by healers, farmers, herbalists, and wisdom keepers who still remember how to call the body back to life. Across two days of virtual immersion, you travel a living landscape of ancestral healing, culminating in a third day on the land: a glorious feast and festival in the woods, where the community celebrates the miracle of healing and honors those who heal the healers.

Why now

A refusal to surrender our bodies

We have all been witness — to the poisoning of our food and water, to the slow violence of a world that forgot how sacred the body is, to the diseases awakened inside us.

And still, you are here. Still breathing. Still searching for a path back to life. WILD! Healing gathers international healers, farmers, herbalists, and physicians not to dominate illness, but to outnumber it with wisdom. It is an infrastructure of survival — a living sanctuary where healing is communal, sacred, and possible.

The Invitation

A love letter to those
who survived illness

Read it slowly. It was written for you.

Beloved,

This is a call to healing. Not the kind that comes only in sterile rooms or printed prescriptions. But the kind that rises from the earth itself. The kind that remembers what our ancestors knew before forgetting became policy. Before healing became industry. Before the language of medicine stopped listening to the wisdom of plants and soil and rivers.

There are ways we have forgotten.

Ways that knew how to cool inflammation with leaves.Ways that knew how to strengthen blood with roots.Ways that knew how to quiet the nervous system with the rhythm of drum and breath.Ways that taught the body how to return.

And now the whisper is rising again. From forests. From farms. From grandmothers who still remember the names of plants. From great-grandmothers whose prayers still hover over our bloodlines.

They whisper across time: You were never meant to heal alone.

This is an invitation. An invitation to step into an infrastructure of survival. A living sanctuary where we begin again. A place where healing is not rushed. Where bodies are listened to. Where symptoms are not dismissed but understood as messages.

A place where we walk with medicine. Where we learn the language of herbs again. Where food returns to its sacred role as nourishment, not harm. Where the soil teaches the body how to regenerate. Where international healers, farmers, herbalists, and physicians gather not to dominate illness, but to outnumber it with wisdom.

A place where we remember that healing is communal. And sacred. And possible.

We call it WILD! Healing.

Not wild in chaos. Wild in the way forests are wild — self-repairing, intelligent, abundant with remedies waiting to be discovered.

Here we resurrect knowledge buried beneath centuries of forgetting. Here we make room for miracles. Here we honor those who have survived illness and those who stand beside them. Here we sit at long tables beneath trees and eat foods that nourish the blood. Here drums echo through the woods and dancers carry the stories of resilience in their feet. Here the healers are honored. And the healers of healers are honored too.

And as the evening lanterns glow between the trees, something ancient happens again. A remembering. You begin to feel it in your chest. A softening. A breath that travels deeper than it has in years.

And perhaps, just perhaps, you hear them. The voices of your grandmothers. The voices of your great-grandmothers. Leaning close to your spirit. Whispering over your life the words that have always belonged to you:

Be well.

So we ask you now — gently, with reverence for everything you have endured:

Are you ready to be well?

If you are — come walk with us. Come walk with the medicines. Come walk toward the miracle of your own healing.

The Journey

— the day you set out for the land

Day three moves from embodiment to the road. This is the journey itself — you travel to the land, body settled for the trip, arriving where the gathering unfolds in person on September 12. Now you set out for the woods — settled, awake, and holding what you chose to bring.

A word for the road

Why the land is medicine

A truth carried down through Indigenous lineage, to hold as you travel: that the land is not a backdrop to healing, but the healer itself.

the land as ancestor the spiritual side of healing grief as medicine reciprocity with the earth

In the days of old, when a person lost their way, the elders would say: go back to the forest. Walk with the medicines. Listen again. Day three is the day you begin to walk.

You do not travel to the land to be healed by it. You travel to remember you were never separate from it.

An offering of your own

A way to honor someone

After all you have received across Day One and Day Two, the journey opens a ritual that is yours alone. A seed or a tree to plant, or a small thing you hold dear — brought to the land in honor of a name:

a seed, for someone who didn't make it a tree, for someone healing right now a keepsake, in honor of yourself
  • name the one it is for, and why
  • keep it close as you travel
  • let what you feel travel alongside it
  • plant it, or leave it, on the land

What will you bring, and whose name will you speak into the soil?

Preparing the body for the journey

Keep your nervous system regulated

A pilgrimage is asked of the body as much as the spirit. We know there's a flight to catch, a ride to arrange, a train to board. Before you close the screen and set out, you tend the nervous system so you travel settled, open, and awake:

grounding & breathwork ancestral prayer for the road setting an intention for arrival

This day is a pilgrimage, a medicine walk across time and continents; you travel regulated, holding what you chose to bring, ready to arrive not as a guest but as one coming home.

The journey to the land is just as sacred as the land itself.

Speaker

Dr. Jesse Stoff

MD, MDH, FAAFP · Integrative Physician & Clinical Immunologist

Dr. Jesse A. Stoff is an integrative physician and clinical immunologist who has spent more than three decades focused on the immune system and its role in healing. He earned his BA in biology, cum laude, from Adelphi University, and his MD from New York Medical College in 1981 — followed by postgraduate training that included a family-practice residency year and a fellowship in emergency medicine, and postdoctoral study in clinical medicine and homeopathy at the Royal London Homeopathic Hospital in London, England.

He is founder and Medical Director of Integrative Medicine of New York (formerly Linchitz Medical Wellness) on Long Island, where the work centers on immunotherapy for autoimmune disease, onco-immunology for cancer, and preventative medicine. His approach draws on clinical immunology and the principle of biochemical individuality, integrating conventional and holistic modalities to help restore immune function and the body’s own capacity to heal. He has taught integrative immunology within Dr. Andrew Weil’s Integrative Medicine Fellowship at the University of Arizona and consulted for Cancer Treatment Centers of America.

Beyond his practice, Dr. Stoff founded the Stoff Institute for Medical Research, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit devoted to public education, patient advocacy, and consultation on immune-system disorders and immune reconstitution. He is a Fellow of the American Academy of Family Physicians and a Fellow of the American College of Longevity Medicine, an elected member of the Male Breast Cancer Coalition’s Clinical Advisory Board, and serves on the executive board of the New York Cancer Resource Alliance.

A prolific author, he is best known for the bestselling Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: The Hidden Epidemic and The Prostate Miracle, and more recently the medical text Clinical Immunology, Volume 1: Integrative Onco-Immunology — reflecting a career-long emphasis on integrative, immune-centered care.

  • Fellow, American College of Longevity Medicine
  • Founder & Medical Director, Integrative Medicine of New York
  • Founder, Stoff Institute for Medical Research (501c3)
  • Focus: clinical immunology, integrative onco-immunology, autoimmune & preventative medicine

Speaker

Dr. Dieudonné Jean-Baptiste

MD, MSc, CFMP · Functional & Longevity Medicine

Dr. Dieudonné Jean-Baptiste is a Haitian physician and the founder and lead physician of La Clinique PHI — Priorité Santé Internationale — a functional, integrative and longevity medicine practice in Pétion-Ville, in metropolitan Port-au-Prince. He holds a Doctor of Medicine and a Master of Science in Public Health from the Université de Montréal, and is a Certified Functional Medicine Practitioner (CFMP).

His clinical philosophy is rooted in what he calls la médecine des causes — the medicine of root causes. Rather than treating disease by name, PHI seeks to understand why the body has lost its capacity to repair itself, and to help it recover that capacity. Conventional medicine, as he frames it, excels at managing crises and relieving symptoms; but for chronic conditions such as diabetes, hypertension and persistent fatigue, his work asks why the problem appeared in the first place — with an emphasis on the foundations of health: sleep, stress, movement and nutrition.

At La Clinique PHI, care follows a structured method of evaluation, treatment, reassessment and follow-up, guided by three stated values: scientific excellence, transparency and education, and integrity and empathy. The clinic frames its mission as transforming health through the medicine of causes, under the tagline “La médecine, autrement !” — “Medicine, differently.”

Dr. Jean-Baptiste is an active public voice for functional medicine in Haiti, featured by the Haitian outlet Juno7 and interviewed on LE POINT, the current-affairs program of Métropole Télé, on his root-cause approach to chronic disease and healthy longevity. He also writes for the clinic’s blog, teaching on topics from detoxification to metabolic health.

  • Founder & lead physician, La Clinique PHI (Priorité Santé Internationale), Pétion-Ville, Haiti
  • MD · MSc, Public Health, Université de Montréal
  • Certified Functional Medicine Practitioner (CFMP)
  • Practices functional / integrative / longevity (“root-cause”) medicine
  • Public advocate for functional medicine in Haitian media (Juno7; LE POINT / Métropole Télé)

Draft · D-08 · Naming the dedicated sites — 3 options

Naming the dedicated sites

Right now the subtitle (a space dedicated in the luminary’s honor) reads like it’s their own title or organization. Three ways to make the relationship clear:

Option 1 · Labeled

A tiny label sets the site apart from a job title. Minimal, and easy to read at a glance.

Option 2 · Rephrased

No extra label — the wording itself carries the meaning. Cleanest, but each line gets a little longer.

Option 3 · Place marker

A divider and small-caps set the site as a named place / plaque, distinct from a title. Most decorative.

— three more —
Option 4 · Chip badge

A small pill categorises the line as a space, not a role. Reads instantly, adds a touch of colour.

Option 5 · Plaque bar

The site sits on a clay band at the card’s base, like an engraved plaque — clearly a separate thing.

Option 6 · Whisper note

Keeps the site line as-is and adds a faint one-line note beneath that spells out the relationship.

Day Three · September 11 · On the road

The Journey

— the day you set out for the land

Day three moves from embodiment to the road. This is the journey itself — you travel to the land, body settled for the trip, arriving where the gathering unfolds in person on September 12. Now you set out for the woods — settled, awake, and holding what you chose to bring.

A word for the road

Why the land is medicine

A truth carried down through Indigenous lineage, to hold as you travel: that the land is not a backdrop to healing, but the healer itself.

the land as ancestor the spiritual side of healing grief as medicine reciprocity with the earth

In the days of old, when a person lost their way, the elders would say: go back to the forest. Walk with the medicines. Listen again. Day three is the day you begin to walk.

You do not travel to the land to be healed by it. You travel to remember you were never separate from it.

An offering of your own

A way to honor someone

After all you have received across Day One and Day Two, the journey opens a ritual that is yours alone. A seed or a tree to plant, or a small thing you hold dear — brought to the land in honor of a name:

a seed, for someone who didn't make it a tree, for someone healing right now a keepsake, in honor of yourself
  • name the one it is for, and why
  • keep it close as you travel
  • let what you feel travel alongside it
  • plant it, or leave it, on the land

What will you bring, and whose name will you speak into the soil?

Preparing the body for the journey

Keep your nervous system regulated

A pilgrimage is asked of the body as much as the spirit. We know there's a flight to catch, a ride to arrange, a train to board. Before you close the screen and set out, you tend the nervous system so you travel settled, open, and awake:

grounding & breathwork ancestral prayer for the road setting an intention for arrival

This day is a pilgrimage, a medicine walk across time and continents; you travel regulated, holding what you chose to bring, ready to arrive not as a guest but as one coming home.

The journey to the land is just as sacred as the land itself.

Draft · The Remembering (copy)
The remembering

In the days of old, when a person lost their way, the elders would say: "Go back to the forest. Walk with the medicines. Listen again."

WILD! Healing is a return to that ancient remembering. It is not simply a program. It is a pilgrimage — a medicine walk across time and continents, guided by healers, farmers, herbalists, and wisdom keepers who still remember how to call the body back to life. Across two days of virtual immersion, you travel a living landscape of ancestral healing, culminating in a third day on the land: a glorious feast and festival in the woods, where the community celebrates the miracle of healing and honors those who heal the healers.
